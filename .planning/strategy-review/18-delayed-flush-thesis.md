# Adversarial Review: "Delayed Flush" Pattern After Hot CPI/PCE

**Claim**: When CPI/PCE prints hot but the market holds on data day, the real sell-off comes 1-3 days later as "hopium fades." Monday is the specific flush window.

**Verdict**: Narrative masquerading as a pattern. The empirical base rate is too low to trade on, the mechanism is untestable, and the Monday specificity is false precision.

---

## 1. Base Rate -- Markets That Hold Usually Keep Holding

Academic literature on macro data surprises (Balduzzi, Elton, Green 2001; Gilbert et al. 2017) consistently finds that bond and equity markets absorb CPI/PCE information within minutes to hours, not days. When equities hold after a hot print, the dominant outcome is that the market accepted the data -- not that it is storing up a delayed reaction. Studies of SPX behavior on CPI surprise days show that the close-to-close direction on data day has weak-to-zero predictive power for the following 1-3 sessions. The "delayed flush" is a low-base-rate event being treated as the expected outcome.

## 2. Information Absorption -- The Evidence Is Mixed, But Favors Speed

The macro information absorption literature splits into two camps. Under-reaction papers (Bianchi, Lettau, Ludvigson 2022) find that macro data takes 1-2 months to fully price into equities through earnings revisions and policy path adjustments. Over-reaction papers (Ai and Bansal 2018) show markets initially gap too hard and mean-revert. Neither camp supports a 1-3 day "delayed flush" -- the under-reaction operates on a much longer horizon, and the over-reaction suggests the data-day move itself was too large. A trader betting on a Monday crash after a Friday hold is in a no-man's-land between the two effects.

## 3. "Hopium Absorbs Bad Data" -- Unfalsifiable

The specific mechanism -- that ceasefire optimism masks the market's true reaction to hot data, which then surfaces days later -- cannot be tested. If the market drops Monday, the thesis claims vindication. If it rallies Monday, the thesis says "hopium held longer." If it sells off Wednesday instead, "delayed flush took longer." There is no outcome that disproves the claim, which means it has no predictive content. Compare this to a testable version: "When CPI surprises hot by >0.1% and SPX closes flat or green, SPX is down >0.5% within 3 sessions at least 60% of the time." That is falsifiable. The narrative version is not.

## 4. Survivorship Bias -- You Remember the Crashes

September 13, 2022: Hot CPI, SPX dropped 4.3% on the day -- no delayed flush, immediate carnage. October 13, 2022: Hot CPI, market reversed from deep red to close green -- then rallied for weeks. February 14, 2023: Hot CPI, held flat, next three sessions were mixed-to-up. The "delayed flush" cases exist but are cherry-picked from a much larger sample where markets either reacted immediately or shrugged it off permanently. Memory bias does the rest -- dramatic delayed sell-offs are vivid and memorable; non-events are forgotten.

## 5. Monday Specificity -- False Precision on a Vague Thesis

Why Monday and not Tuesday? The thesis provides no mechanism for Monday-specific selling. Weekend risk premium argues the opposite -- Monday gaps tend to be random relative to Friday data. Adding day-of-week precision to a qualitative thesis does not make it more rigorous; it makes it more likely to be wrong by narrowing the window where the prediction must hit. If forced to test this, the sample of "hot CPI Friday + flat close + Monday selloff >0.5%" events is so small it cannot reach statistical significance.

---

**Bottom Line**: The "delayed flush" is a narrative, not a pattern. It fails the base-rate test, the testability test, and the sample-size test. Position sizing should assume the data-day reaction is the reaction, not a head-fake.
